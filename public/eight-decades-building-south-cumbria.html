Eight Decades of Building South Cumbria: What's Changed, What Hasn't

Published December 2025 6 min read By the Leck Construction Team

Leck Construction was founded in Barrow-in-Furness in 1946. The town was rebuilding from the war; the company started small, doing the kind of jobs the moment demanded. Eighty years on, we're still based in Barrow, still independently owned, and still building across South Cumbria and the Lake District.

Eighty years is unusual in this industry. Most construction businesses don't reach a third generation. The ones that do all share something — and the things that have kept Leck going through eight decades aren't the things that have changed. They're the things that haven't.

What's changed: almost everything technical

The materials, methods, regulations and tools of construction have transformed completely since 1946:

  • The materials revolution. Lime to cement and back to lime; single-skin masonry to cavity wall to insulated panel; timber sash to uPVC to high-spec timber composite. The frame, the envelope, the finishes — all of it has been reinvented several times over.
  • Health and safety. The Construction (Design and Management) regulations alone have rewritten how every project is planned. Falls from height — long the leading cause of fatalities on UK construction sites — have reduced very significantly across our industry over recent decades, partly through better equipment but mostly through better planning.
  • Energy and environment. From buildings designed without thought for energy use to buildings that must meet stringent thermal performance and carbon targets before they can be approved. The role of the contractor has expanded with the role of the building.
  • Procurement. From informal handshake contracts on a typed letter to the JCT, NEC and FIDIC suite of contracts, with their attendant programmes, change-control processes and dispute resolution mechanisms.
  • Digital tools. BIM (Building Information Modelling), drones for site surveys, GPS-guided plant, cloud-based programme tracking, digital snagging — all standard on commercial projects today and unimaginable in 1946.

Every one of these changes was a step forward. The buildings we deliver now are safer, more durable, and more comfortable to use than the buildings we delivered fifty years ago. The sites are safer to work on. The relationships with clients are clearer.

What hasn't changed: the things that matter

Despite all of that — and we've adopted all of it where it adds value — the things that determine whether a construction business survives have stayed the same:

Clients want predictability

The biggest project in their year shouldn't be the biggest source of stress in their year. The contractor who delivers consistently on programme and budget is the contractor who gets the next phase, and the one after that. Our partnerships with GlaxoSmithKline (since 1997) and BAE Systems (since 2006) have lasted because every job, large or small, has been delivered the same way: planned thoroughly, communicated clearly, executed without surprises.

People want to work somewhere that respects them

Construction has historically had a difficult relationship with its workforce. Our experience says the opposite: skilled, well-treated tradespeople produce better work, faster, and stay around longer. Most of our site managers have been with us for over 15 years. That stability shows up in the finished product in ways that less experienced clients don't always recognise but consistent clients always do.

Local matters

Our work is concentrated in South Cumbria for a reason. We know the conditions, the building stock, the local supply chain. Operatives don't waste hours commuting. Materials don't travel halfway across the country. And we're held accountable in our own community — every project we complete is one our team will drive past for the rest of their working lives.

That accountability shapes every decision. There are no second chances on a high-profile project in a market town. Word travels fast in South Cumbria, and a contractor who damages a building or walks off a site can take a generation to rebuild trust.

"Word travels fast in South Cumbria, and a contractor who damages a building or walks off a site can take a generation to rebuild trust."

Reputation is built over decades

Construction is a small industry. We've held an NHBC Grade A rating, ISO 9001 and ISO 14001 certifications, and our long-term partnerships have lasted because we deliver consistently and we're easy to work with. Those are the things prospective clients can verify before they ever pick up the phone.

The 80-year rule

Eighty years is enough time to have outlasted many fashions in construction. We've watched contractors come and go who specialised in the latest delivery model — design and build, traditional, two-stage, framework, partnering, alliance — and we've adapted to each. But the underlying business hasn't changed: build well, treat people fairly, deliver what you promise, and stay in your community.

If we're still here in 2046 — and we have every intention of being — those will still be the things that have stayed the same.
